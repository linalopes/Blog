It started with a pile of cardboard I needed to get rid of.

Not a research hypothesis. Not a design brief. Just material in abundance, sitting in the corner of my studio at School of Tomorrow's AI, and the growing feeling that I should do something with it before I drowned in it.

Arduino Day 2026 was coming up. I had done it before — a decade ago, actually, with Women Techmakers in São Paulo, where I handed soldering irons to a room full of programmers and watched them discover that hardware is also writable. The date was a hook, a community anchor, a reason to show up on a map that already existed. I had the cardboard. I had the questions I'd been sitting with about paper as a material for electronics. I had peers who work with the same themes. I had a Twitch channel that was mostly gathering dust.

So I did what I sometimes do without naming it: **I combined everything I already had into something that didn't exist yet.** No grant. No institutional framework. Just the logistics of interests.

<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">What happens when your research material is also your recycling problem? When the community calendar is also your publication date? When your teaching practice is also your prototype?</mark>

The event was called *Paper as Interface*. Three hours, hybrid, free, in-person in Wetzikon and live on Twitch. Three prototypes in one afternoon: paper folds (structural, just scissors), basic electronics embedded in a cardboard system of tabs, and finally Arduino — where I asked the audience whether we even needed it, and what "control" actually means once you've already made a light turn on with wire and a battery.

It worked. Technically and conceptually, it was a round event.

But it didn't find its audience.

---

A friend who runs workshops — the kind that fill up quickly, that people immediately understand — makes things about the web. Apps. Interfaces that people touch every day, on their phones, on their laptops. When she says "I'm running a workshop on X," people already know what X is. They can place themselves inside the experience before it starts.

Creative technologies don't have that. **Paper circuits, physical computing, cardboard as a structural material for electronics — these aren't things most people bump into in daily life.** They're not like ceramics, where everyone has touched clay at least once. They're not like web design, where everyone has wondered how a page actually works.

So there are two possible moves. One is to find the public that already knows this territory exists — makers, educators, designers, architects who prototype with their hands. The other is a longer game: making this field ordinary enough that people seek it out the way they seek out a weekend pottery class.

I don't know yet which move is mine. Probably both, at different scales, at different times.

---

What I do know is that the most interesting thing that came out of the afternoon wasn't the Arduino. It was the system of tabs.

During the session, we built a flat cardboard circuit — what I've been calling the *Z Circuit* — and then transformed it into a three-dimensional structure using folded and interlocking tabs. No glue. No screws. Just cardboard, designed to hold itself. And suddenly something that was lying flat on a table had volume, presence, weight.

That's the thing I want to keep working on. **Not the electronics alone, not the programming alone — but the junction between structure and conductivity.** A kit that designers, architects, educators, and makers could use as a starting point. Something modular. Something that doesn't require soldering to begin.

The Möbius strip I used as an icebreaker for the online audience felt related. One surface, no inside, no outside. Paper that refuses the categories you bring to it. That's roughly what I want the kit to do — refuse the division between craft, electronics, and programming by being all three at once, quietly.

---

The hybrid format worked, with caveats. Good microphone. Two cameras — the laptop camera and a webcam aimed at the table surface. For next time, I'd swap one of the webcams for something that can track and reframe without manual intervention. The technical challenge of designing for a physical space and a digital audience simultaneously is real, and I haven't fully solved it.

But the deeper challenge isn't the camera angle. It's the question that every event like this eventually produces: **who is this for, and how do they find out it exists?**

I don't have an answer yet. Only the next prototype, and a pile of cardboard that's slightly smaller than it was yesterday.

> *What fields have you worked in that people don't know exist yet? How did you build the audience — or did the audience eventually build itself?*
